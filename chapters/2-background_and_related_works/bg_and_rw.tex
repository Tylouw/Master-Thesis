\chapter{Background and Related Works} \label{ch:state-of-the-art}

\section{Technical Foundations}\label{sec:tech_foundations}

\subsection{Robotic insertion as a contact-rich manipulation problem}

Robotic insertion is a common assembly task in which one part is moved into a corresponding opening, such as a peg into a hole or a component into a fixture. Although the desired final state is geometrically defined, the execution is not purely a positioning problem. Small deviations in position or orientation can already lead to contact between the parts \cite{xuCompareContactModelbased2019,tangAutonomousAlignmentPeg2016}. The resulting interaction forces and torques can then determine whether the insertion continues smoothly, requires correction, or fails \cite{raibertHybridPositionForce1981b,whitneyHistoricalPerspectiveState1987,feiContactJammingAnalysis2004b}.

For this reason, robotic insertion is commonly treated as a contact-rich manipulation task. During the insertion motion, the robot may transition from free-space motion to one-sided or multi-point contact. Larger pose errors can produce multi-point contact conditions \cite{tangAutonomousAlignmentPeg2016}, and under such conditions reliable execution often requires compliance, force control, or explicit alignment strategies \cite{raibertHybridPositionForce1981b,whitneyHistoricalPerspectiveState1987,xuCompareContactModelbased2019}. Contact may guide the part into the opening, but it can also lead to sliding, scraping, wedging, or jamming depending on the geometry, tolerance, surface properties, and relative alignment \cite{feiContactJammingAnalysis2004b,wangContactForceTorque2018,beltran-hernandezLearningForceControl2020}. Because of that, so called force/torque maps and contact models have been investigated as a way to relate pose errors to corresponding wrenches during assembly \cite{dietrichContactModelsAssembly2010,abdullahForceTorqueData2015}.

This physical interaction makes insertions relevant for sensor based analysis. Pose information alone does not necessarily describe the contact condition, whereas force and torque signals provide direct information about the current collision and its progression over time \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014,magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018}. In this thesis, insertions are therefore treated as a contact-rich process that produces characteristic multivariate time series signals during successful and failed attempts \cite{magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018,beltran-hernandezLearningForceControl2020}.

\subsection{Sequential sensor information in robotic insertion}

Because the interaction during insertion evolves over time, the task naturally produces sequential sensor information. Relevant signals can include force and torque measurements, robot TCP pose, TCP velocity, joint positions and velocities, and additional sensing. Especially, multi-axis force/torque sensors provide three force components and three torque components and are therefore well suited for observing mechanical interaction during assembly \cite{liMultiaxisForceTorque2025}. In peg-in-hole tasks, such measurements are used to detect contact, estimate deviation, and to improve insertion alignment \cite{tangAutonomousAlignmentPeg2016,abdullahForceTorqueData2015}.

The important point is that these signals are informative not only as isolated values, but also through their temporal development. Contact onset, sliding, correction motions, jamming, or successful task completion can appear as characteristic changes in the recorded sequences. This is reflected in work on contact-state monitoring, where wrench and pose trajectories are used to recognize intermediate assembly states \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014}. A similar idea is used in contact-pose identification, where a sequence of contact measurements is used to infer the relative pose between peg and hole under uncertainty \cite{jinContactPoseIdentification2021a}. More generally, haptic sensing studies have shown that force/torque information can support classification of more general manipulation tasks \cite{chanEffectsForceTorqueTactile2019}.

This sequential view is also reflected in the design of recent robotic datasets. In insertion-specific work, force/torque and pose signals have been recorded to learn from multi-shape insertion attempts \cite{magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018}. Broader contact-rich manipulation datasets combine several synchronized modalities, including vision, robot proprioception, force/torque, audio, and action information \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025,fangRH20TComprehensiveRobotic2024}. Recent work on auditory and audio-visual robot learning further suggests that contact-rich manipulation can benefit from modalities beyond vision and robot state alone \cite{thankarajThatSoundsRight2023,mejiaHearingTouchAudioVisual2024b}.

In this thesis, each insertion attempt is therefore represented as a multivariate time series. Each sensor channel describes one aspect of the robot or process state, while the whole sequence describes how these values evolve during the insertion attempt. The temporal evolution of one sequence is then used to distinguish successful from failed insertions.

\subsection{Formulation as a multivariate time series classification (MTSC) problem}

Time series classification describes the task of assigning a discrete label to an ordered sequence of measurements \cite{fawazDeepLearningTime2019,faouziTimeSeriesClassification2024}. In the multivariate case, each sequence contains several synchronized channels rather than a single signal \cite{bagnallUEAMultivariateTime2018,ruizGreatMultivariateTime2021}. A recorded insertion attempt can therefore be represented as a sequence $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}$, where $T$ denotes the number of time steps and $C$ the number of sensor channels. The corresponding class label $y$ describes the outcome of the trial, for example successful or failed insertion.

This formulation is suitable for robotic insertions, because the relevant information is often distributed across multiple channels. A classifier does not only evaluate individual force, torque, or motion values, but learns patterns in their development over time and in their interaction across channels \cite{fawazDeepLearningTime2019,faouziTimeSeriesClassification2024}. This differs from a static threshold-based decision in which only selected values or hand-crafted features are used. Closely related robotics work already follows this principle when time dependent wrench and pose information are used for contact-state recognition, contact-pose identification, or success related inference \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014,jinContactPoseIdentification2021a,chanEffectsForceTorqueTactile2019}.

% The classification perspective also provides a structured way to evaluate generalization. Standard benchmark archives and large-scale comparative studies emphasize the importance of clearly defined train, validation and test splits and comparable evaluation procedures \cite{dauUCRTimeSeries2019,bagnallUEAMultivariateTime2018,ruizGreatMultivariateTime2021}. In robotic insertion, trials can additionally be grouped by experimental factors such as geometry, tolerance, gripper configuration, or imposed deviations. In this thesis, such group-based splits are used to test whether a model has learned general signal patterns of successful and failed insertions, or whether its performance depends strongly on conditions already seen during training.

In this thesis, the insertion data is therefore treated as a supervised multivariate time series classification problem. Each trial is represented by synchronized sensor channels over a fixed observation window, and the classifier is trained to predict the binary insertion outcome. This formulation connects the physical insertion process to established time series classification methods and provides the basis for the benchmark design used in the following chapters.

\section{Related work on time series classification}

Time series classification has been studied with a wide range of methods, including distance-based classifiers, feature-based methods, ensembles, convolutional neural networks, and transformer-based models \cite{bagnallGreatTimeSeries2017,faouziTimeSeriesClassification2024}. Comparative benchmark studies have shown that no single representation is optimal for all datasets, which motivated methods that combine different views of the same time series \cite{bagnallGreatTimeSeries2017}. Such views may include, for example, raw whole-series, informative intervals (for example mean, std, slope of intervals), local shapelets (characteristic patterns), or dictionary-based pattern (bag-of-words) \cite{bagnallGreatTimeSeries2017}. Later work also showed that deep learning and randomized convolutional transformations can achieve highly competitive performance while reducing the need for manually designed features \cite{fawazDeepLearningTime2019,dempsterROCKETExceptionallyFast2020,ismailfawazInceptionTimeFindingAlexNet2020}.

\subsection{HIVE-COTE 2.0 \cite{middlehurstHIVECOTE20New2021}}

HIVE-COTE 2.0 is a heterogeneous ensemble for time-series classification. The name stands for Hierarchical Vote Collective of Transformation-based Ensembles. Instead of relying on a single representation of the signal, it combines classifiers that operate on different transformed views of the data. In HIVE-COTE 2.0 is the successor of HIVE-COTE, which include "phase-independent shapelets, bag-of-words based dictionaries and phase-dependent intervals." \cite{middlehurstHIVECOTE20New2021} classifiers. The new version replaces these classifiers with the Temporal Dictionary Ensemble, Diverse Representation Canonical Interval Forest and adds the "Arsenal" based on different ROCKET classifiers. This design follows the observation that discriminative information in time series can appear in different forms, such as local subsequences, repeated symbolic patterns, informative intervals, or phase-dependent shapes.

The main strength of HIVE-COTE 2.0 is its classification accuracy. Middlehurst et al. report that HIVE-COTE 2.0 is significantly more accurate on average than the previous state of the art on 112 univariate UCR datasets and 26 multivariate UEA datasets. For this reason, HIVE-COTE 2.0 is an important reference point when discussing the upper end of classical TSC performance. However, this accuracy comes at the cost of a large ensemble structure and substantial computational effort \cite{middlehurstHIVECOTE20New2021,dempsterROCKETExceptionallyFast2020}. In the context of this thesis, HIVE-COTE 2.0 is therefore most useful as a high-performance literature reference, whereas simpler and more scalable methods are more practical for benchmarking on robotic insertion data.

\subsection{ROCKET \cite{dempsterROCKETExceptionallyFast2020}}

ROCKET (RandOm Convolutional KErnel Transform) is based on a different idea. Instead of learning convolutional filters through gradient-based optimization, it applies a large number of randomly generated convolutional kernels to the input time series. From the resulting feature maps, two aggregate features are extracted for each kernel: the maximum value and the proportion of positive values. These transformed features are then used to train a linear classifier, typically ridge regression or logistic regression \cite{dempsterROCKETExceptionallyFast2020,ruizGreatMultivariateTime2021}.

The relevance of ROCKET lies in the combination of simplicity, speed, and strong performance. Dempster et al. show that random convolutional kernels can achieve state-of-the-art accuracy with a fraction of the computational expense of existing methods. Ruiz et al. further identify ROCKET as a recommended choice for multivariate TSC because of its high overall accuracy and remarkably fast training time on the UEA archive \cite{ruizGreatMultivariateTime2021}. This makes ROCKET an important baseline for applied TSC problems. For robotic insertion data, ROCKET is relevant because force, torque, and motion signals may contain local temporal patterns that can be captured by convolutional filters, while the final classifier remains comparatively simple.

\subsection{InceptionTime \cite{ismailfawazInceptionTimeFindingAlexNet2020}}

InceptionTime represents a deep convolutional approach to time series classification. It adapts the Inception architecture, originally developed for image recognition \cite{szegedyGoingDeeperConvolutions2014}, to one-dimensional temporal signals. The model uses several convolutional filters with different kernel sizes in parallel, which allows it to extract temporal patterns at multiple scales. The final method is an ensemble of deep convolutional models built from stacked Inception modules.

This architecture is relevant because robotic insertion signals may contain both short contact events and longer process-level patterns. Force peaks, oscillations, or brief alignment events may occur in a small time window, whereas the overall progression from approach to contact and final insertion evolves over a longer sequence. A multi-scale convolutional model such as InceptionTime is therefore a meaningful deep learning baseline for this type of data. In addition, Ismail Fawaz et al. show that InceptionTime is on par with HIVE-COTE in terms of accuracy while being much more scalable.
% It therefore provides a useful contrast to transformer-based models because it learns temporal features through convolutions rather than attention.

\subsection{PatchTST \cite{nieTimeSeriesWorth2023b} and transformer-based time series models}

Transformer models were originally introduced for better language translation through attention mechanisms \cite{vaswaniAttentionAllYou2017}. In time series learning, attention-based models are attractive because they can relate information from distant time steps without recurrent processing. However, applying standard transformers directly to long sequences is computationally expensive because self-attention scales quadratically with the number of input tokens \cite{vaswaniAttentionAllYou2017,nieTimeSeriesWorth2023b}.

Transformer-based methods for multivariate time series classification were already explored before PatchTST. For example, gated transformer networks were proposed to model both channel-wise and step-wise correlations in MTSC by merging two transformer towers with a gating mechanism \cite{liuGatedTransformerNetworks2021}. PatchTST addresses the scaling issue from a different angle by dividing a time series into patches before applying the transformer encoder. Instead of treating every individual time step as one token, short subsequences (patches) are used as input tokens. According to Nie et al., patching retains local semantic information, reduces the time and memory complexity of attention, and allows the model to attend over longer history. PatchTST also uses a channel-independent design, in which each channel is treated individually, while using the same embedding and transformer weights. This design is particularly relevant for multivariate data in general, because it separates temporal representation learning within each channel from later aggregation across channels.

The original PatchTST work focuses primarily on long-term forecasting and self-supervised representation learning rather than classical time series classification. Nevertheless, its architectural ideas are very relevant for classification tasks with long multivariate sequences. In the context of robotic insertion, patching can summarize local events such as contact transients or short force responses, while the transformer can relate how these events evolve over a full insertion. This makes the model attractive for insertion force/torque data, where relevant information is often distributed across local temporal intervals rather than isolated single samples. In this thesis, PatchTST is therefore not used for forecasting but adapted for binary sequence classification. The original forecasting head is replaced by a binary classification head, because the objective is to assign one success or failure label to the complete trial. In addition, the original channel-independent design is not adopted without further consideration. This aspect is particularly interesting because the force/torque and other robotic signal channels can be interpreted either as separate time series channels or as parts of one mechanically coupled insertion signal. For this reason, the role of channel interaction is treated as an explicit modeling question and will be investigated further in the benchmark. For these reasons, PatchTST is used in this thesis as the main model architecture for classifying successful and failed insertion attempts from multivariate sensor time series.

\section{Related robotic insertion and assembly datasets}

In the following, related datasets are presented and their differences explained in task scope, sensing modalities, label structure, and intended use relative to the dataset created in this thesis. The related datasets can be divided into insertion-specific dataset, sequence-based insertion recognition and classification work, and broader contact-rich assembly and manipulation datasets.

\subsection{Insertion-specific dataset}

The closest insertion-specific reference is the experimental force/torque dataset for robot learning of multi-shape insertion by De Magistris et al. (\figref{fig:related_works} (a)) \cite{magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018}. The dataset contains peg-in-hole insertion data with force/torque and pose information for multiple variations of convex-shaped pegs \cite{magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018}. The authors show that the dataset can be used to train a robot to insert polyhedral pegs into holes using only six-axis force/torque measurements as inputs, and also for related contact tasks such as shape recognition \cite{magistrisExperimentalForceTorqueDataset2018}. This makes it highly relevant to the present thesis because both works treat force-based insertion as a data-driven learning problem. However, the focus differs. De Magistris et al. emphasize learning insertion behavior and shape-related contact information, whereas this thesis focuses on classification of successful and failed insertion attempts under controlled variations of geometry, tolerance, gripper configuration, and imposed deviations.

\subsection{Sequence-based insertion recognition and classification work}

Several works are closely related to insertion datasets even when their main contribution is a recognition method rather than a benchmark. Jasim and Plapper record wrench and pose trajectories for different contact states in force-guided peg-in-hole assembly and use these signals to recognize the current contact condition \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014}. Jin et al. classify contact poses from a sequence of contact measurements in order to infer the relative pose between peg and hole under uncertainty \cite{jinContactPoseIdentification2021a}. Similarly, Abdullah et al. model force/torque data for contact position estimation during peg-in-hole assembly \cite{abdullahForceTorqueData2015}. Yan et al. propose a supervised contact-state recognition model based on force and torque data, coupled with adaptive impedance control for peg-in-hole assembly at different inclination angles (\figref{fig:related_works}(b)) \cite{yanLearningbasedContactStatus2021}. More recent work also uses time series force information for contact-state identification in inclined-hole insertion \cite{shenLearningbasedRobotAssembly2025} and for hole-finding or deviation prediction on flexible support platforms (\figref{fig:related_works} (c)) \cite{xiongDeepLearningbasedHolefinding2026}.

These studies show that insertion-related force and pose trajectories contain characteristic information that can be used for classification. At the same time, their task formulation differs from the one used in this thesis. Most of them aim to recognize intermediate contact states, contact poses, or deviation-related quantities to support alignment and control \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014,jinContactPoseIdentification2021a,abdullahForceTorqueData2015,yanLearningbasedContactStatus2021,shenLearningbasedRobotAssembly2025,xiongDeepLearningbasedHolefinding2026}. In contrast, the dataset in this thesis is designed around complete insertion trials under defined deviations and assigns an outcome label to each attempt. This produces a benchmark setting that is closer to trial-level success/failure classification than to online contact-state estimation.

A related distinction appears in more general haptic success-classification work. Chan et al. study how force/torque and tactile sensing modalities support classification of manipulation success \cite{chanEffectsForceTorqueTactile2019}. This is close to the objective of this thesis in regards of success-classification, but it is not specific to robotic insertion and does not provide a benchmark around testing insertion-related distribution shifts.

\subsection{Broader contact-rich assembly and manipulation datasets}

Beyond insertion-specific work, several recent datasets address contact-rich manipulation and assembly on a broader scale. REASSEMBLE (\figref{fig:related_works} (d)) is a multimodal dataset for robotic assembly and disassembly built around the NIST Assembly Task Board 1 \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025,AssemblyPerformanceMetrics2018}. It includes four actions, namely pick, insert, remove, and place, involving 17 objects and 4,551 demonstrations, of which 4,035 are successful \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025}. The recorded modalities include multi-view RGB cameras, event cameras, force/torque sensing, microphones, and robot proprioception \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025}. It also provides annotations for tasks such as hierarchical temporal action segmentation, motion policy learning, and success or anomaly detection \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025}. REASSEMBLE is therefore highly relevant as a modern contact-rich assembly dataset, but its scope is broader than the focused insertion setting of this thesis.

RH20T (\figref{fig:related_works} (e)) represents an even broader view of robot manipulation data. It contains over 110,000 real-world robot manipulation sequences and corresponding human demonstrations across a large set of contact-rich skills, robots, and environments \cite{fangRH20TComprehensiveRobotic2024}. Each sequence includes multimodal visual, force, audio, and action information, and the dataset emphasizes diversity in tasks, robot configurations, and environments \cite{fangRH20TComprehensiveRobotic2024}. Such a dataset is important for multimodal robot learning and general representation learning. However, its purpose is not to provide a targeted benchmark for insertion outcome classification.

FurnitureBench (\figref{fig:related_works} (f)) is another important assembly benchmark, but with a different emphasis. It focuses on real-world long-horizon furniture assembly and provides more than 200 hours of teleoperated demonstrations, 3D-printable furniture models, and a simulation environment \cite{heoFurnitureBenchReproducibleRealworld2025a}. Its strength lies in benchmarking complex manipulation policies over extended assembly sequences. The benchmark explicitly includes challenging subtasks such as inserting and screwing, which require accurate alignment and dexterous control \cite{heoFurnitureBenchReproducibleRealworld2025a}. Nevertheless, FurnitureBench is not primarily designed as a sensor-level time series classification dataset for individual insertion attempts.

\section{Positioning of this thesis}

The reviewed works show that robotic assembly data serve different purposes. Some studies focus on contact-state or contact-pose recognition during insertion \cite{jasimContactstateMonitoringForceguided2014,jinContactPoseIdentification2021a,yanLearningbasedContactStatus2021}. Others provide broader multimodal datasets for manipulation, assembly, or policy learning \cite{sliwowskiREASSEMBLEMultimodalDataset2025,fangRH20TComprehensiveRobotic2024,heoFurnitureBenchReproducibleRealworld2025a}. The dataset developed in this thesis is positioned between these directions. It is smaller than large-scale manipulation datasets, but more directly structured around trial-level insertion outcome classification from multivariate sensor time series.

The contribution of this thesis is therefore not only a new set of insertion trials, but a dataset and benchmark structure for supervised multivariate time series classification in contact-rich robotic insertion. By recording complete insertion attempts with synchronized sensor channels and controlled experimental factors, the dataset supports both outcome classification and analysis of generalization across task variations. This distinguishes it from broader manipulation datasets and from insertion studies focused mainly on control, alignment, or intermediate contact-state recognition.
