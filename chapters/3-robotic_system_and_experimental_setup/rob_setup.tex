\chapter{Robotic System and Experimental Setup}\label{ch:rob_setup}

This chapter describes the physical experimental platform used for the data acquisition in this thesis. The setup consists of an industrial robot manipulator, a parallel gripper with interchangeable finger configurations, multiple insertion rods, a custom taskboard containing the matching geometries, and a loadcell-based success detection mechanism mounted below the taskboard. Together, these components form the basis for the automated execution and evaluation of insertion attempts. While this chapter focuses on the hardware configuration and the functional role of each component, the data acquisition procedure and the dataset generation pipeline are described in the next Chapter~\ref{ch:dataset}.

\section{Robotic System Overview}\label{sec:rob_setup-overview}

The experimental setup is built around a UR5e robotic manipulator equipped with a Robotiq Hand-E gripper to its endeffector. The robot repeatedly picks-up a rod from the holder, which is builtin into the taskboard stand, and inserts it into the corresponding hole, while recording all relevant data. A photo of the full setup is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:setup_overview}, and a schematic overview of the main components is provided in Figure~\ref{fig:setup_schematic}.

For the experiments the UR5e robot was selected due to its ease of use, broad availability combined with sufficient accuracy to achieve repeatable cartesian motion to perform the insertions. Since the robot itself is not the subject of investigation in this work, only its functional role within the setup is considered here. Similarly, the Robotiq Hand-E gripper is used to reliably grasp and release the insertion rods and furthermore allow different shapes of rods to be grasped due to interchangeable and fully customizable fingers. Different finger configurations were designed to ensure safe grasping of the respective rod types while also allowing little play to generate diverse training data. The specific rod and finger geometries are described in Section~\ref{sec:rods-fingers}.

The robot interacts with a custom taskboard mounted next to it. The taskboard contains the respective insertion holes with a movable loadcell underneath to detect successful insertions, which enables automated success detection during data acquisition. The taskboard design is described in Section~\ref{sec:taskboard}, while the loadcell-based success detection system is presented in Section~\ref{sec:loadcell}.

\section{Insertion Rods and Gripper Fingers}\label{sec:rods-fingers}

The insertion tasks consists of a set of custom insertion rods with different cross-sectional geometries. In total 3 rod types are used: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Small Rod with $8.7$ mm diameter
    \item Big Rod with $16.7$ mm diameter
    \item Rectangular Rod with side length $12\times8$ mm
\end{enumerate}
These shapes were picked to represent common insertion conditions while still providing comparable results. Figure~\ref{fig:rods_overview} shows the 3 rods used in this work. All rods are $50$ mm long and machined out of steel.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

\end{figure}

To grasp the different shaped rods, three fingers were designed with different purposes in mind regarding the later experiments. The first finger type (Round) can be seen in \figref{fig:finger_designs} (a) and is used with its triangular indent for grasping both the Small Rod and Big Rod. The second finger type (Rect) in \figref{fig:finger_designs} (b) consists of a rectangular indent used for grasping only the Rectangular Rod. Both finger types (Round and Rect) were used in the first and second data acquisition run. The third finger type (Flat) in \figref{fig:finger_designs} (c) is a generic type used for grasping both round and rectangular rods for the third data acquisition run.

In addition to the rod-based insertion features, the taskboard also includes mounting holes for 3 snap-fit experiments, shown in \figref{fig:taskboard_cad} (b) at the top. These features were part of the original taskboard design as snap-fit was initially considered as a second task family. However, snap-fit trials are not included in the final dataset produced and used in this thesis. The main reason for this decision was to keep the scope of data acquisition manageable. Especially, reliable semi-automatic label assignment is more straightforward for the rod insertion tasks, since a successful insertion can be detected automatically by the loadcell system described in Section~\ref{sec:loadcell}. For snap-fit tasks, defining a similar success criterion would have required additional sensing or validation steps. Therefore, the final experiments in this thesis focus only on rod insertion tasks.

\section{Loadcell-Based Success Detection}\label{sec:loadcell}

To enable semi-automated label assignment during data acquisition, the setup includes a loadcell mounted underneath the taskboard. The purpose of this subsystem is to detect whether an insertion rod has passed through its corresponding hole far enough to mechanically contact the loadcell beneath the board. In the experimental logic of this work, this contact is interpreted as a successful insertion, when readout value exceeds a tuned threshold. Semi-automatic means, that when an insertion fails (fail criteria are described in \textcolor{red}{TODO}) the human robot operator has to manually pick up the rod and insert it in the predefined holder shown in \figref{fig:taskboard_legs} (c).

In \figref{fig:loadcell_concept} (a) the loadcell's placement underneath the hole of rect\_rod\_t0.3 is shown, while \figref{fig:loadcell_concept} (b) shows all components and how they are connected. The loadcell is screwed on to a 3D-printed bracket, which is then fixed to the table.

The loadcell used in this work is a RS PRO Lavprofil Tryk Vejecelle rated for $0.06$ kg to $3$ kg (labelled as "Loadcell" in \figref{fig:loadcell_concept} (b)) and is connected to a dedicated loadcell analog-to-digital converter, namely Load Cell click, which uses the HX711 chip (labelled as "ADC" in \figref{fig:loadcell_concept} (b)). The converter is interfaced with an Arduino Uno R4 WiFi (labelled as "Arduino" in \figref{fig:loadcell_concept} (b)), which samples the sensor signal at approximately $10$ Hz. The measured loadcell value is transmitted wirelessly to the recording computer, where it is monitored during data acquisition.

The loadcell subsystem is included specifically to avoid purely manual success annotation and to provide reproducible results for successful insertion, as human errors would introduce inconsistencies into the dataset during long recording sessions.
